Realism emerged as a major artistic and literary movement in mid-19th-century Europe, as a reaction to Romanticism’s excessive focus on emotions and lack of rationality. Rejecting all things Romantic, Realism offered a truthful display of everyday life, ordinary people, and present social issues without exaggeration.

The centre of Realism focused on working-class and middle-class subjects, urban and rural scenes, corrupt and complicated reality, accurately detailed representations, social commentary on poverty, labour, and inequality, and treating all classes equally. Realism depicted humans in real settings by impacting literature and making art more democratic.

In literature, Realism became especially important through novelists who depicted society with psychological depth and social awareness. Writers such as Charles Dickens, Robert Browning, Thomas Hardy and Leo Tolstoy depicted the routines, struggles, and moral complications of ordinary people, often situating their narratives within recognizable social and historical contexts. Rather than heroic or imagination-based stories, realist literature focused on realistic events, detailed settings, and characters shaped by their environment and social conditions. Through this approach, Realism broadened the scope of literature, transforming it into a medium for exploring the structures of everyday life and the realities of modern society.
