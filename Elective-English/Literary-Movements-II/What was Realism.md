Realism emerged as a major artistic and literary movement in mid-19th-century Europe, primarily as a direct counterpart to Romanticism’s emotional excess and idealization. It began around the 1840s in France, led by artists like Gustave Courbet amid social upheavals from the Industrial Revolution and political shifts. Rejecting all things Romantic, Realism sought truthful depictions of everyday life, ordinary people, and contemporary social issues without accentuations or embellishments.

The core characteristics of Realism focused on working-class subjects, urban and rural scenes, unglamorous and complex reality, accurately detailed representations, social commentary on poverty, labour, and inequality, and treating all classes equally. Influencing literature, democratising art, and paving the way for Impressionism by prioritizing observation over imagination, Realism helped portray unidealized humans in real settings.

In literature, Realism became especially significant through novelists who depicted society with psychological depth and social awareness. Writers such as Charles Dickens, Robert Browning, Thomas Hardy and Leo Tolstoy portrayed the routines, struggles, and moral complications of ordinary people, often situating their narratives within recognizable social and historical contexts. Rather than heroic or fantasy-bssed plots, realist literature emphasized imaginable events, detailed settings, and detailed characters shaped by their environment and social conditions. Through this approach, Realism broadened the scope of literature, transforming it into a medium for examining the structures of everyday life and the realities of modern society.
