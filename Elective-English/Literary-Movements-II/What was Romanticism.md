Romanticism was an important artistic, literary, and intellectual movement that began in Europe in the late eighteenth century and became very influential in the early nineteenth century. It marked a major change in the way people thought about art, literature, and human experience. During this time, writers and artists started moving away from strict rules, order, and logical thinking that had dominated the earlier age known as the Enlightenment. Instead, they began to focus more on human emotions, imagination, and personal expression. Because of this shift in thinking and feeling, Romanticism gradually became the spirit of the age, shaping the culture and ideas of the time.

The movement developed partly as a reaction against the strong emphasis on reason and rationality during the Enlightenment. Enlightenment thinkers valued logic, science, and universal rules that could explain the world. At the same time, the Industrial Revolution was transforming society with factories, machines, and rapid urban growth. Many Romantic writers and artists felt that this new mechanical and rule-bound world ignored the deeper emotional and spiritual sides of human life. In response, they challenged these ideas and turned their attention toward feelings, imagination, creativity, and the uniqueness of each individual. The influence of Romanticism can be clearly seen in the works of many famous artists and writers such as William Wordsworth and Samuel Taylor Coleridge.

Overall, Romanticism encouraged artists and writers to express their true feelings and ideas freely. It emphasized creativity, imagination, and the importance of the individual voice. The movement had a lasting impact on literature, art, and music, and it also influenced modern ideas about creativity, personal freedom, and the value of emotional expression in human life. Even today, many works of art and literature continue to reflect the Romantic belief that imagination and emotion are essential parts of understanding the world.
