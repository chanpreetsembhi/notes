Modernism in literature was a major movement that emerged in the late 19th and early 20th centuries, particularly after the shock of World War I. It marked a break from previous forms, structures, and themes that had been around earlier periods like Realism and Romanticism. Writers began to feel that traditional ways of storytelling could no longer accurately represent the complicated modern life. As a result, Modernist literature has characteristics like experimentation and a deep questioning of already present norms and beliefs at the time. It reflects a world that seemed chaotic, uncertain, confusing and one that did not have a clear moral direction.

Modernist writing focuses on what people think and feel inside their minds. Writers like James Joyce, T.S. Eliot, Friedrich Nietzsche, and Karl Marx show thoughts as they happen, using simple and direct ways. They care more about feelings and ideas than actions. Because of this, readers must think more to understand the story. Modernist writing also shows that people feel alone and confused. War, big changes in society, and loss of old beliefs made people feel lost. Writers show a broken world where people feel empty, and stories often end without clear answers.

In addition to themes, Modernism also changed the way people wrote. Writers like Ezra Pound told others to make writing new and different. They did not want to follow old rules. They used new styles and simple words to show ideas. This made Modernism very important, and it changed literature a lot. It also helped new styles like Postmodernism to grow. Overall, Modernism changed how stories show real life and ideas.
