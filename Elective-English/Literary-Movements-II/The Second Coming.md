William Butler Yeats’ *The Second Coming* presents a dark and disturbing vision of a world falling apart after a great disorder in society. The poem begins with the image of a falcon flying in widening circles, no longer able to hear its falconer(trainer or owner of the falcon bird). This symbolizes a loss of control and order, suggesting that traditional systems of authority—moral, political, and social—are breaking down. Yeats describes a world where “things fall apart” and “the centre cannot hold,” emphasizing chaos and instability. Violence and anarchy are spreading everywhere, and the sense of unity that once held civilization together has completely fallen.

As the poem continues, Yeats portrays a society affected by confusion and moral decay. He compares the “best,” who lack conviction, with the “worst,” who are full of passion. This reversal of values shows a troubling reality where good people are passive and ineffective, while destructive/bad forces are active and dominant. The imagery of a “blood-dimmed tide” drowning innocence portrays the idea that brutality has overtaken purity and goodness. The poem suggests that humanity is entering a terrifying new phase where ethical boundaries no longer exist, and the world is dominated by fear and uncertainty.

In the second half of the poem, Yeats introduces a prophetic and almost apocalyptic vision. He refers to a “Second Coming,” which is a reference to the Christian belief in the return of Jesus Christ. However, instead of a hopeful or transformational event, Yeats imagines something far more evil. He describes a disturbing creature emerging from the desert, with the body of a lion and the head of a man, moving slowly toward Bethlehem. This figure is not a saviour but a symbol of a new, monstrous era about to begin. The use of dream-like and symbolic imagery gives the poem a sense of certainty, as if this dark transformation of humans cannot be stopped.

The poem concludes with a powerful and haunting question about what kind of being is about to be born into the world. Yeats imagines this “rough beast” heading towards Bethlehem to be born, suggesting the coming of a new age defined by violence, cruelty, and disorder. Rather than offering hope, the poem leaves readers with a sense of dread and uncertainty about the future. Overall, *The Second Coming* reflects Yeats’s fear that human history moves in cycles, and that the collapse of one era, by default, gives rise to another, which is often a more terrifying phase.
