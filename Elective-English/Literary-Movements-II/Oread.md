Hilda Doolittle’s poem *Oread* is a short and powerful Imagist poem that blends the natural elements of the sea and the forest. The speaker is an oread, a mountainous nymph creature from Greek mythology, who calls upon the sea to rise and merge with the land. Through vivid imagery, the poem creates a striking fusion of two different landscapes.

In the first part of the poem, the oread is described as talking to the sea directly, commanding it to “whirl up” and move with energy and force. The language is imagist, suggesting motion and intensity. The sea is not calm or passive; instead, it is portrayed as something wild and powerful, capable of transformation.

As the poem goes on, the sea begins to take on the qualities of a forest. The waves are described as “pointed pines,” and the water is imagined as if it were a dense woodland. This combination of images reflects the Imagist focus on clear, sharp, and solid visual impressions rather than abstract ideas that cannot be picturized. The poem continues to build this connection between sea and forest, focusing sharply on the unity of natural forces. The oread seems to desire a kind of merging or overlap between the two environments, suggesting that nature is interconnected rather than divided into separate parts.

In conclusion, *Oread* captures a moment of intense imaginative vision where boundaries between different elements of nature blend together. Through its concise language and vivid imagery, the poem becomes a good example of Imagist principles by presenting a clear, immediate picture that evokes both movement and harmony in the natural world.
